Dorothea wants to do something "great," and instead spends the book compromising, quietly, into the texture of an ordinary marriage. Eliot isn't mocking her — it's closer to a tender kind of clear-sightedness: most people's ideals end up dissolving, undramatically, into the specifics of daily life.

> "for the growing good of the world is partly dependent on unhistoric acts; and that things are not so ill with you and me as they might have been, is half owing to the number who lived faithfully a hidden life, and rest in unvisited tombs."

That line made me think of writing my thesis — no one will ever remember how a particular draft got revised, but it's exactly those invisible revisions that add up to whatever ends up presentable.

**Question to sit with:** if "greatness" was never the destination but just a byproduct of the process, is the "lack of meaning" I keep circling actually the wrong question to be asking?
